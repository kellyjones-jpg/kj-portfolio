Metoposaurus 3D Animation

Using Autodesk Maya, I created a 3D animation of a prehistoric amphibian for the Sam Noble Museum of Natural History's Prehistoric Visions exhibition.

The name Metoposaurus means "front lizard," but despite its reptilian-sounding name, it was actually a massive amphibian—closely resembling a modern-day giant salamander but growing up to 10 feet in length. It thrived in a bayou-like environment, where water was essential for its survival.

To capture this prehistoric world, I used Autodesk Maya to create a 3D animation that immerses viewers in a shallow underwater setting. In my animation, the Metoposaurus primarily stays near the bottom, swimming just below the surface without breaking through—a behavior that helped it avoid predators. By closely studying its movements and ecological adaptations, I aimed to bring this ancient amphibian to life with scientific accuracy and artistic interpretation.

My animation was featured in Prehistoric Visions, an exhibition that explored the deep connection between art and Vertebrate Paleontology. This exhibit showcased original works by students from the OU School of Visual Arts, each inspired by fossils from the Sam Noble Museum's Vertebrate Paleontology Collection. Many of these fossils were discovered in Oklahoma and the American Southwest, offering a glimpse into the region's prehistoric past through artistic expression.
